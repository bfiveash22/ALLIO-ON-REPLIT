# Psychedelic-Assisted Healing Research

## Overview

This document compiles current research on psychedelic-assisted healing, focusing on three compounds with the most robust clinical evidence: psilocybin, MDMA, and ketamine. It is intended as an educational resource for FFPMA members seeking to understand the therapeutic potential, mechanisms of action, and safety considerations of these substances within a healing framework.

> **Important Legal & Safety Notice:** The legal status of psychedelic substances varies by jurisdiction. Psilocybin and MDMA remain Schedule I controlled substances under U.S. federal law as of this writing, though regulatory pathways are advancing. Ketamine is a Schedule III substance available through licensed medical providers. This document is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice or encouragement to use controlled substances. Members should consult qualified legal and medical professionals regarding any therapeutic use.

## 1. Psilocybin-Assisted Therapy

### 1.1 Background

Psilocybin is the primary psychoactive compound found in over 200 species of fungi (commonly known as "magic mushrooms"). Upon ingestion, psilocybin is converted to psilocin, which acts primarily as a 5-HT₂A serotonin receptor agonist.

### 1.2 Mechanism of Action

**Neurochemical:**
- **5-HT₂A agonism**: Primary mechanism; activates serotonin 2A receptors in the prefrontal cortex
- **Default Mode Network (DMN) disruption**: Psilocybin reduces activity and connectivity in the DMN, the brain network associated with self-referential thinking and the "ego"
- **Neuroplasticity enhancement**: Promotes dendritic spine growth and synaptogenesis
- **Glutamate release**: Secondary glutamate release in cortical pyramidal neurons

**Proposed Healing Mechanism:**
1. DMN suppression allows dissolution of rigid thought patterns
2. Increased neural connectivity creates opportunities for new pattern formation
3. Enhanced neuroplasticity allows lasting structural brain changes
4. Emotional processing becomes accessible without defensive filtering

### 1.3 Clinical Evidence

**Major Depression:**
- **COMPASS Pathways Phase IIb** (2022): 233 patients with treatment-resistant depression. Single 25mg dose produced significant improvement at 3 weeks; response rate 29.1% vs 7.6% placebo.
- **Johns Hopkins** (2020): 24 patients with major depressive disorder. Two psilocybin sessions produced rapid, large reductions in depression scores; 71% showed >50% improvement at 4 weeks.
- **UCSF/Imperial College** (2021): Psilocybin compared to escitalopram (SSRI). Psilocybin showed comparable efficacy with faster onset and broader improvements in well-being.

**End-of-Life Anxiety:**
- **NYU Langone** (2016): 29 cancer patients. Single psilocybin dose produced immediate and sustained decreases in anxiety and depression; 80% still showed clinically significant improvement at 6.5 months.
- **Johns Hopkins** (2016): 51 cancer patients. High-dose psilocybin produced large decreases in clinician-rated and self-rated measures of depression, anxiety, and death anxiety.

**Addiction:**
- **Johns Hopkins (Tobacco)** (2014): 15 smokers received psilocybin-assisted therapy. 80% were biologically confirmed abstinent at 6 months (vs. ~35% for best available treatments).
- **NYU (Alcohol)** (2022): 93 participants with alcohol use disorder. Psilocybin group showed 83% reduction in heavy drinking days vs 51% for placebo.

### 1.4 Therapeutic Protocol Framework

A typical clinical psilocybin therapy protocol includes:

| Phase | Duration | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Screening** | 1-2 sessions | Medical/psychiatric evaluation, informed consent |
| **Preparation** | 2-3 sessions | Therapeutic alliance building, intention setting, education about the experience |
| **Dosing Session** | 6-8 hours | Psilocybin administration (25-30mg) in controlled setting with trained therapists |
| **Integration** | 3-6 sessions | Processing insights, developing action plans, maintaining gains |
| **Follow-up** | Months | Ongoing integration support, community connection |

**Setting Requirements:**
- Comfortable, aesthetically pleasing room
- Eye shades and curated music playlist
- Two trained therapists present throughout
- Medical monitoring equipment available
- No digital devices during the session

### 1.5 Safety Profile

- **Physiological safety**: Psilocybin has very low physiological toxicity; no known lethal dose in humans
- **Psychological risks**: Challenging experiences ("bad trips"), potential triggering of latent psychosis in predisposed individuals
- **Contraindications**: Personal or family history of psychotic disorders, current lithium use, certain cardiovascular conditions
- **Dependency potential**: Very low; psilocybin is not considered addictive and produces rapid tolerance

## 2. MDMA-Assisted Therapy

### 2.1 Background

MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine) is an empathogen-entactogen that primarily increases serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine release while also releasing oxytocin. Originally synthesized in 1912, it gained therapeutic attention in the 1970s-80s before being scheduled in 1985.

### 2.2 Mechanism of Action

**Neurochemical:**
- **Serotonin release**: Massive serotonin release (via SERT reversal) produces emotional warmth and empathy
- **Oxytocin release**: Enhances social bonding, trust, and emotional openness
- **Amygdala modulation**: Reduces fear response while maintaining emotional awareness
- **Prefrontal-amygdala connectivity**: Enhances top-down emotional regulation

**Proposed Healing Mechanism:**
1. Reduced fear and defensiveness allows traumatic memories to be accessed safely
2. Enhanced empathy and self-compassion facilitates processing of shame and guilt
3. Strengthened therapeutic alliance through oxytocin-mediated trust
4. Emotional memories can be reconsolidated without the associated terror

### 2.3 Clinical Evidence

**Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD):**
- **MAPS Phase III Trial 1** (MAPP1, 2021): 90 participants with severe PTSD. 67% of MDMA group no longer met PTSD criteria at 18-week endpoint (vs. 32% placebo). Published in Nature Medicine.
- **MAPS Phase III Trial 2** (MAPP2, 2023): 104 participants. Results consistent with MAPP1, though FDA requested additional analysis regarding functional unblinding concerns.
- **Long-term follow-up**: Phase II data showed maintained benefits at 12-month follow-up, with 67% of responders maintaining remission.

**Other Indications Under Investigation:**
- Social anxiety in autistic adults (Danforth et al., 2018)
- Alcohol use disorder (Phase II recruiting)
- Eating disorders (Phase II recruiting)
- Couples therapy for PTSD (Monson et al., ongoing)

### 2.4 Therapeutic Protocol Framework

| Phase | Duration | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Screening** | 1-2 sessions | Medical evaluation, PTSD assessment, informed consent |
| **Preparation** | 3 sessions | Therapeutic alliance, psychoeducation, coping skills |
| **Dosing Session 1** | 8-10 hours | MDMA administration (80-120mg + optional 40-60mg supplement), with two trained therapists |
| **Integration** | 3 sessions | Processing, integration of insights between dosing sessions |
| **Dosing Session 2** | 8-10 hours | Second MDMA session (typically 4 weeks after first) |
| **Integration** | 3 sessions | Continued processing and integration |
| **Dosing Session 3** | 8-10 hours | Third MDMA session (optional, 4 weeks after second) |
| **Final Integration** | 3 sessions | Long-term integration planning, relapse prevention |

### 2.5 Safety Profile

- **Physiological risks**: Hypertension, tachycardia, hyperthermia, hyponatremia (in uncontrolled settings)
- **Neurotoxicity concerns**: Some evidence of serotonergic neurotoxicity with repeated recreational use; clinical protocols use limited sessions with adequate spacing
- **Psychological risks**: Emotional vulnerability during and after sessions, potential for retraumatization if inadequately supported
- **Contraindications**: Uncontrolled hypertension, cardiovascular disease, current SSRI/SNRI use (serotonin syndrome risk), hepatic impairment
- **Dependency potential**: Low in therapeutic settings; moderate risk in recreational contexts

### 2.6 Regulatory Status

- FDA granted "Breakthrough Therapy" designation for PTSD in 2017
- FDA Advisory Committee reviewed Phase III data in June 2024; additional data was requested
- Regulatory pathway continues with sponsor (Lykos Therapeutics) working with FDA on path forward
- Several states and cities have implemented or are considering decriminalization measures

## 3. Ketamine-Assisted Therapy

### 3.1 Background

Ketamine is a dissociative anesthetic that has been used medically since 1970. Its rapid antidepressant effects, discovered in the early 2000s, have led to significant clinical adoption for treatment-resistant depression and other conditions.

### 3.2 Mechanism of Action

**Neurochemical:**
- **NMDA receptor antagonism**: Primary mechanism; blocks glutamate NMDA receptors
- **AMPA receptor potentiation**: Downstream effect that triggers BDNF release and mTOR signaling
- **BDNF increase**: Brain-derived neurotrophic factor promotes synaptic growth and repair
- **Synaptogenesis**: Rapid formation of new synaptic connections, visible within hours
- **Anti-inflammatory effects**: Reduces neuroinflammatory markers (IL-6, TNF-α)

**Proposed Healing Mechanism:**
1. NMDA blockade produces a "reset" of overactive default mode network
2. BDNF-driven synaptogenesis rapidly creates new neural pathways
3. Dissociative state allows perspective shift on entrenched psychological patterns
4. Anti-inflammatory effects address neuroinflammation associated with depression

### 3.3 Clinical Evidence

**Treatment-Resistant Depression:**
- **Esketamine (Spravato)**: FDA-approved in 2019 for treatment-resistant depression. Multiple Phase III trials demonstrated rapid (within hours to days) antidepressant effects.
- **IV Ketamine**: Zarate et al. (2006) landmark study showed 71% response rate within 24 hours in treatment-resistant depression. Hundreds of subsequent studies confirm rapid antidepressant effects.
- **Oral/Sublingual Ketamine**: Growing evidence supports at-home ketamine protocols under medical supervision, with several telemedicine platforms offering guided treatment.

**Suicidal Ideation:**
- Ketamine produces rapid (within hours) reduction in suicidal ideation, making it valuable for acute crisis intervention
- Esketamine received specific FDA indication for depressive symptoms with suicidal ideation in 2020

**Chronic Pain:**
- Low-dose IV ketamine infusions show efficacy for Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS), fibromyalgia, and neuropathic pain
- Mechanism involves NMDA receptor modulation of central sensitization

**Other Applications:**
- PTSD (emerging evidence, several RCTs showing benefit)
- Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (preliminary evidence)
- Substance use disorders (several trials showing benefit for alcohol and cocaine dependence)
- Anxiety disorders (emerging evidence)

### 3.4 Therapeutic Protocol Framework

**IV Ketamine Protocol (Standard Clinical):**

| Phase | Duration | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Evaluation** | 1 session | Psychiatric assessment, medical screening, informed consent |
| **Induction Series** | 2-3 weeks | 6 infusions at 0.5 mg/kg over 40 minutes, typically Mon/Wed/Fri |
| **Integration** | Ongoing | Psychotherapy sessions between and after infusions |
| **Maintenance** | Monthly | Booster infusions as needed (typically monthly, then tapering) |

**Ketamine-Assisted Psychotherapy (KAP):**

| Phase | Duration | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Preparation** | 1-2 sessions | Intention setting, therapeutic alliance building |
| **Medicine Session** | 2-3 hours | Sublingual or IM ketamine with psychotherapeutic support |
| **Integration** | 1-2 sessions | Processing and applying insights |
| **Series** | 6-12 sessions | Full treatment course with ongoing assessment |

### 3.5 Safety Profile

- **Physiological risks**: Transient hypertension, nausea, dizziness; risk of bladder toxicity with chronic/heavy use
- **Psychological risks**: Dissociation, anxiety, perceptual disturbances
- **Abuse potential**: Schedule III controlled substance; dependency can develop with regular use
- **Contraindications**: Uncontrolled hypertension, raised intracranial pressure, active psychosis, pregnancy
- **Monitoring requirements**: Blood pressure monitoring during infusions; periodic liver and bladder function tests for ongoing use

### 3.6 Accessibility

Ketamine is currently the most accessible psychedelic-adjacent therapy:
- Available through licensed ketamine clinics nationwide
- Esketamine (Spravato) available through certified REMS healthcare settings
- At-home ketamine programs available via telemedicine (with medical oversight)
- Covered by some insurance plans (particularly esketamine)

## 5. Harm Reduction and Safety Framework

### 5.1 Universal Precautions

For any psychedelic-assisted healing approach:

1. **Medical screening**: Full medical and psychiatric evaluation before any psychedelic use
2. **Set and setting**: Mental preparation (set) and physical/social environment (setting) critically influence outcomes
3. **Trained support**: Trained therapists or guides should be present during experiences
4. **Integration**: Post-experience integration with qualified therapists is essential for lasting benefit
5. **Medication interactions**: Screen for dangerous interactions (MAOIs, lithium, SSRIs with MDMA)
6. **Contraindicated populations**: Personal/family history of psychotic disorders, certain cardiac conditions, pregnancy

### 5.2 Emergency Protocols

In the event of a difficult experience:

- **Grounding techniques**: Physical grounding (feet on floor, holding objects), breathing exercises
- **Reassurance**: Remind the individual that the experience is temporary and they are safe
- **Change of setting**: Adjust music, lighting, or room if needed
- **Medical intervention**: Have benzodiazepines available for severe anxiety; monitor vitals
- **Post-experience support**: Ensure follow-up care and integration support

### 5.3 Ethical Considerations

- **Informed consent**: Full disclosure of risks, benefits, and legal status
- **Therapist boundaries**: Clear professional boundaries during vulnerable states
- **Cultural sensitivity**: Respect for indigenous traditions and cultural contexts of psychedelic use
- **Equity**: Access to psychedelic therapies should not be limited by socioeconomic status
- **Ongoing research**: Support for continued rigorous clinical research

## 6. Future Directions

### 6.1 Emerging Research Compounds

- **DMT/Ayahuasca**: Research into rapid-acting neuroplastic effects; trials for depression
- **5-MeO-DMT**: Preliminary evidence for treatment-resistant depression and substance use
- **Ibogaine**: Research into opioid addiction interruption; cardiac safety concerns being addressed
- **LSD**: Renewed research into microdosing and full-dose therapy for anxiety, depression, and cluster headaches
- **Mescaline**: Limited but growing research into therapeutic applications

### 6.2 Regulatory Landscape

- Oregon (Measure 109): Licensed psilocybin therapy centers operating since 2023
- Colorado (Proposition 122): Regulated access framework in development
- FDA breakthrough therapy designations accelerating clinical research
- Multiple international jurisdictions advancing psychedelic therapy regulation
- Growing scientific consensus supporting rescheduling of psilocybin

### 6.3 Technology Integration

- **Neuroimaging**: fMRI and EEG monitoring during psychedelic sessions for personalized protocols
- **Digital therapeutics**: App-based integration support and outcome tracking
- **AI-assisted integration**: Machine learning tools to support post-experience processing
- **Virtual reality**: VR-enhanced preparation and integration sessions
