# IV Therapy Standards & Protocols

## Overview

Intravenous (IV) nutrient therapy delivers vitamins, minerals, amino acids, and other therapeutic agents directly into the bloodstream, bypassing the digestive system for maximum bioavailability. This document establishes clinical standards for commonly administered IV protocols within the FFPMA network.

## General Standards

### Facility Requirements
- Clean, temperature-controlled treatment room
- Emergency equipment readily accessible (crash cart, AED, epinephrine)
- Proper waste disposal for sharps and biohazardous materials
- Refrigeration for heat-sensitive compounds
- Patient monitoring equipment (blood pressure cuff, pulse oximeter)

### Pre-Treatment Assessment
- Complete medical history and current medication review
- Allergy assessment (especially to preservatives, sulfites)
- Baseline labs recommended:
  - Complete metabolic panel (CMP)
  - Complete blood count (CBC)
  - G6PD screen (required before high-dose vitamin C)
  - Magnesium (RBC)
  - Iron studies
- Informed consent documentation
- Pregnancy test for women of childbearing age

### Administration Standards
- Sterile technique for all IV preparations
- Verify patient identity before administration
- Double-check solution contents and concentrations
- Monitor vital signs: baseline, 15 minutes, and post-infusion
- Document adverse reactions immediately
- Maintain IV log with batch numbers and expiration dates

## Protocol 1: Myers' Cocktail

### Standard Formula
| Component | Dose | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Magnesium chloride | 2–5 mL (20%) | Monitor for warmth/flushing |
| Calcium gluconate | 2–4 mL (10%) | Slow push |
| Vitamin B12 (hydroxocobalamin) | 1 mL (1,000 mcg/mL) | |
| Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) | 1 mL (100 mg/mL) | |
| Vitamin B5 (dexpanthenol) | 1 mL (250 mg/mL) | |
| B-Complex | 1 mL | |
| Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) | 5–10 mL (500 mg/mL) | |
| Normal saline | 100–250 mL | Dilution vehicle |

### Administration
- Infusion rate: 30–60 minutes
- Frequency: Weekly for 4–8 weeks, then monthly maintenance
- Monitor for magnesium-related warmth and flushing

### Indications
- Chronic fatigue, fibromyalgia
- Acute asthma exacerbation
- Migraine headaches
- Upper respiratory infections
- Seasonal allergies
- Athletic performance and recovery
- General wellness optimization

## Protocol 2: High-Dose Vitamin C

### Pre-Requisite
- G6PD test MUST be performed before first high-dose vitamin C infusion
- G6PD-deficient patients are contraindicated for doses above 15 g

### Dosing Schedule (Graduated)
| Session | Dose | Infusion Time | Saline Volume |
|---------|------|---------------|---------------|
| 1 | 15 g | 45–60 min | 250 mL |
| 2 | 25 g | 60–90 min | 500 mL |
| 3 | 50 g | 90–120 min | 500 mL |
| 4+ | 75–100 g | 2–3 hours | 1,000 mL |

### Monitoring During High-Dose Infusion
- Blood glucose monitoring (vitamin C can interfere with glucometer readings)
- Hydration status
- Urine output
- Blood pressure and heart rate every 30 minutes
- Osmolality check for doses > 50 g

### Indications
- Immune support during acute illness
- Adjunctive cancer care (under oncologist coordination)
- Chronic infections (Lyme, EBV, viral)
- Wound healing support
- Oxidative stress reduction

### Contraindications
- G6PD deficiency
- Renal insufficiency (GFR < 30)
- Iron overload conditions (hemochromatosis)
- Oxalate nephropathy history

## Protocol 3: NAD+ Infusion

### Standard Protocol
| Component | Dose | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| NAD+ | 250–500 mg (standard) | Start low, titrate up |
| NAD+ | 750–1,000 mg (intensive) | For addiction/neuro recovery |
| Normal saline | 500–1,000 mL | Slow infusion critical |

### Administration
- Infusion rate: 2–6 hours depending on dose (SLOW is essential)
- Too-rapid infusion causes chest tightness, nausea, cramping
- Start at lowest drip rate and increase as tolerated
- Patient should eat before infusion
- Frequency: Daily for 5–10 days (loading), then monthly maintenance

### Indications
- Anti-aging and longevity
- Neurodegenerative conditions
- Addiction recovery
- Chronic fatigue
- Athletic performance
- Cognitive enhancement

### Side Effects (Rate-Related)
- Chest pressure or tightness (slow the rate)
- Nausea, abdominal cramping
- Headache
- Muscle cramping
- All resolve with rate reduction

## Protocol 4: Glutathione Push

### Standard Protocol
| Component | Dose | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Glutathione (reduced) | 1,200–2,000 mg | IV push over 10–15 min |
| Normal saline flush | 10 mL | Before and after |

### Administration
- Typically administered at the end of a Myers' Cocktail or vitamin C infusion
- Slow IV push over 10–15 minutes
- May also be given via nebulizer for respiratory conditions
- Frequency: 1–3x weekly

### Indications
- Detoxification support
- Heavy metal chelation adjunct
- Liver disease support
- Parkinson's disease (adjunctive)
- Skin brightening and anti-aging
- Post-chemotherapy recovery
- Mold illness recovery

### Precautions
- Sulfite-sensitive patients: use preservative-free formulations
- May cause transient headache if pushed too rapidly
- Monitor for rare anaphylactoid reactions

## Protocol 5: Chelation Therapy (EDTA/DMPS)

### CaEDTA Chelation
| Component | Dose | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Calcium disodium EDTA | 1–3 g | Based on body weight/kidney function |
| Vitamin C | 5–10 g | Antioxidant support |
| Magnesium | 2 g | Mineral replacement |
| B-Complex | 1 mL | Cofactor support |
| Normal saline | 500 mL | |

### Administration
- Infusion time: 1.5–3 hours
- Frequency: Weekly for 20–40 sessions
- Renal function monitoring every 5–10 sessions
- Mineral replacement between sessions is critical
- Post-chelation mineral replenishment protocol required

### Indications
- Heavy metal toxicity (lead, mercury, cadmium, arsenic)
- Cardiovascular disease (TACT trial evidence)
- Peripheral neuropathy from heavy metal exposure

## Emergency Protocols

### Anaphylaxis Response
1. Stop infusion immediately
2. Epinephrine 0.3 mg IM (1:1,000 concentration)
3. Call emergency services
4. Maintain airway, monitor vitals
5. IV fluid bolus
6. Diphenhydramine 50 mg IV
7. Document incident completely

### Vasovagal Response
1. Recline patient, elevate legs
2. Cool compress to forehead
3. Monitor blood pressure
4. Resume infusion at reduced rate once stable

### Infiltration/Extravasation
1. Stop infusion
2. Apply warm or cool compress per compound
3. Elevate affected extremity
4. Document and monitor site
5. Restart at new site if continuing treatment

## Documentation Requirements
- Pre-treatment assessment and vital signs
- Solution preparation log with lot numbers
- Infusion start/stop times and rates
- Patient monitoring notes
- Any adverse events and interventions
- Post-treatment instructions provided to patient
- Follow-up scheduling

## Quality Assurance
- Compounding pharmacy verification for custom formulations
- Temperature log for refrigerated compounds
- Expiration date tracking
- Staff competency annual review
- Incident reporting and review process
